At the Robotics Research Centre, IIIT Hyderabad, our team built a real-time autonomous navigation pipeline on AutoDP — an in-house platform designed to seamlessly bridge AD research and deployment across both simulation and physical vehicles.
The Problem
Testing autonomous driving algorithms typically requires either expensive commercial platforms or starting from scratch each time. AutoDP solves this by providing a modular stack where any navigation or perception algorithm can be plugged in within minutes — on a real car or in CARLA simulation.
What We Built: We mapped the IIIT Hyderabad campus as a 3D point cloud using LiDAR, forming the base map for all navigation and localization. On top of this we built a full point-to-point navigation pipeline capable of:
 
Perception -> Localization -> Global Planning -> Local Planning -> Control  
 
The sensor suite on the vehicle included a 3D LiDAR, monocular and stereo cameras, and CAN bus for actuation. All modules ran within the ROS ecosystem, with LOAM handling LiDAR odometry.
My Role: My work spanned the full pipeline — from setting up the ROS-based integration of individual modules to end-to-end testing on the physical vehicle. This included tuning, debugging across the stack, and validating behaviour in both CARLA simulation and real-world runs.
The Result: The system successfully demonstrated fully autonomous point-to-point navigation across the IIIT Hyderabad campus at up to 15 km/h, handling real-world conditions including varying road surfaces.
 
Working on this project has been an invaluable experience, allowing me to  gain lots of hands-on  experience with cutting-edge technologies and develop a deeper understanding of the complexities involved in real-world robotics research and development.
